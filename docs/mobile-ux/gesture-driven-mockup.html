Design Decision: Browser, Files, and Shell Placement

Chosen: Drawer Quick Actions + Surface Strip

Browser, Files, and Shell live as quick-action buttons at the bottom of the workspace drawer. Tapping one opens it as a new surface in the current pane -- it then appears as a chip in the surface strip at the top. This is the right call for three reasons:

1. These are "open" actions, not "switch" actions. You do not toggle between them constantly. You open a browser tab, use it, then go back to terminal. The drawer is the natural home for "do something new."

2. Once opened, they become surface chips -- the same horizontal strip used for terminal tabs. This means you switch between terminal/browser/files using the same gesture (tap surface chip), which is consistent and learnable.

3. The bottom of the drawer is always visible without scrolling, so these actions are always one swipe + one tap away. Two gestures total. Fast enough for any frequency of use.

Rejected: Top-right overflow menu

Hiding these behind a "..." menu adds an extra tap and hides functionality. Overflow menus are where features go to die. The drawer is already a natural "actions" panel -- no need for a second one.

Rejected: Long-press gesture

Long-press is invisible -- there is no affordance. New users would never discover it. Long-press is acceptable for power-user shortcuts but not for primary navigation to Browser/Files/Shell.

Minimap: Technical Bridge to Desktop

The minimap is not decorative -- it is a live representation of the desktop's LayoutSnapshot. The mobile app subscribes to the desktop's pane tree and renders it faithfully.

Data Source

The minimap reads from LayoutSnapshot (defined in bonsplit). Each PaneGeometry provides a PixelRect with x, y, width, height. The mobile app normalizes these into the minimap viewport, preserving exact proportional relationships. The focusedPaneId determines which pane gets the blue highlight.

Pane Previews

Each minimap pane shows a tiny text preview -- the last 4-6 lines of terminal output. This is fetched via the socket API's screen capture command. Previews update every 2 seconds while the minimap is open (not on every frame). Browser panes show a URL summary instead of terminal text.

Zoom Transition

Tapping a minimap pane triggers a zoom animation: the tapped pane expands to fill the screen while other panes fade out. The animation follows cubic-bezier(0.4, 0, 0.2, 1) over 300ms. The surface strip populates with that pane's tabs during the transition, arriving fully visible by the time the zoom completes.

Edge Cases and States

Empty Workspace (No Panes)

If a workspace has no panes (freshly created), the terminal area shows a centered message: "No panes yet. This workspace is empty on the desktop." with a subtle "Open Shell" button. The minimap pinch-out shows an empty container outline with the same message.

Single Pane (No Minimap Value)

Pinch-out still works but shows a single pane filling the entire minimap viewport. A subtle label reads "1 pane -- split on desktop to see layout here." This teaches the user what the minimap is for without blocking the gesture.

Connection Lost

A thin red bar appears below the surface strip: "Connection lost. Reconnecting..." with a pulsing animation. Terminal content freezes but remains visible (last known state). Modifier keys and keyboard are disabled (grayed out). On reconnect, the bar turns green briefly ("Connected") then fades out over 2 seconds.

Many Surfaces (Strip Overflow)

When a pane has more than ~5 surfaces, the strip scrolls horizontally. A subtle gradient fade on the right edge hints at more chips. A long-press on any chip shows a popover list of all surfaces for quick jumping (power user shortcut).

Landscape Orientation

In landscape, the modifier bar moves to the right edge as a vertical column. Surface strip remains at top. This gives the terminal maximum horizontal space for wide output (logs, diffs, tables). The minimap naturally adjusts its aspect ratio to match the wider viewport.

First Launch / Onboarding

On first launch, after connecting to a desktop cmux instance, a brief overlay teaches the three core gestures: left-swipe (workspaces), pinch-out (minimap), swipe-up (keyboard). Each gesture is demonstrated with a ghost finger animation. The user can skip or tap through. Total time: ~8 seconds.

Design Rationale

Why No Bottom Nav

Bottom navigation bars are designed for apps where you switch between equal-priority sections. cmux mobile is not that app. It is a terminal with occasional excursions to browser or files. The bottom real estate is far more valuable as a modifier key bar -- something terminal users will touch hundreds of times per session. A bottom nav would have been tapped maybe twice.

Why the Drawer (Not a Tab or Sidebar)

Workspaces are high-value but low-frequency. You check them when context-switching, not every few seconds. A persistent sidebar wastes screen width on a narrow phone. A tab adds chrome. A drawer is invisible until needed, then provides full context (branch name, pane count, status) in a generous layout. The left-edge swipe is a universal mobile convention (Android back gesture, iOS navigation) so discoverability is built in.

Why Pinch-Out for Minimap

The pinch-out gesture is semantically perfect: you are literally "zooming out" to see the bigger picture. It maps directly to the mental model of "I want to see my whole desktop layout." The alternative -- a button or menu item labeled "Pane Overview" -- is less discoverable but more importantly less satisfying. The pinch creates a physical, spatial connection between "my phone showing one pane" and "my desktop showing all panes." That spatial continuity is what makes the mobile app feel like a true companion rather than a separate tool.

Why Browser/Files/Shell in the Drawer

These are "open new thing" actions, not "switch to existing thing" actions. The drawer is already the "what else can I do?" panel. Placing them there creates a consistent mental model: left-swipe = access everything that is not the current terminal. Once opened, they become surface chips -- meaning the switching interaction is the same 1-tap gesture regardless of whether you are switching between two terminals or between a terminal and a browser. Consistency wins.
